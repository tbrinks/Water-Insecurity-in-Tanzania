Water Insecurity in Tanzania

Assessing Access and Quality in Informal Urban Aettlements

Thania Brinks

Introduction & Background

Dar es Salaam, Tanzania's largest city, serves as a critical focal point for studying urban water vulnerabilities. Despite its economic significance, rapid urbanization has outpaced municipal development, leading to widespread poverty and the growth of informal urban settlements.

These communities face severe issues with water infrastructure, often relying on a fragmented system of kiosks, private vendors, and unregulated boreholes. Service reliability remains low, with many residents navigating daily uncertainty regarding the safety and availability of their water supply.

Climate Change Impacts

Climate change acts as a threat multiplier in Dar es Salaamâ€™s informal settlements. Increased variability in precipitation and rising sea levels directly impact both water access and water quality.

Heavy flooding events frequently lead to the overflow of pit latrines, facilitating the fecal-oral route of contamination in shallow groundwater and open-air pipes. Conversely, prolonged dry spells stress existing supply chains, increasing the concentration of pollutants. These environmental stressors correlate directly with adverse health outcomes, specifically the seasonal spike in enteric diseases.

The HWISE Scale

The Household Water Insecurity Experiences (HWISE) Scale is a validated tool designed to capture the cross-cultural, experiential dimension of water insecurity. Unlike traditional volumetric measures, HWISE focuses on the frequency of experiences such as worry, anger, and lifestyle disruptions related to water.

By assessing the adequacy, availability, accessibility, and reliability of water through a behavioral lens, the scale allows researchers to identify insecurity that standard infrastructure audits might overlook, particularly in low-resource urban settings.

Objective

Assess the success of the Household Water Insecurity Experiences Scale (HWISE) in identifying water insecurity in low-resource areas with existing water insecurity in Dar es Salaam, Tanzania.

Methodology

This study was conducted across three major districts in Dar es Salaam: Kinondoni, Ilala, and Temeke.

  • Collaboration: Survey development was a joint effort with Muhimbili University of Health and Allied Sciences (MUHAS).
  • Sample Size: 364 households were surveyed.
  • Administration: Surveys were administered in Kiswahili by trained MUHAS students.

Discussion: The Resilience Paradox

A stark discrepancy exists between the HWISE findings and water access metrics collected in this study. While HWISE suggests 98% security, the high prevalence of enteric illness and significant travel distances indicate a more profound insecurity.

When compared to JMP/WHO definitions of water security, which prioritize "safely managed" services (on-premises, available when needed, and free from contamination), 70% of the study population would be classified as water insecure. This discrepancy suggests that in environments of chronic scarcity, residents may "normalize" extreme burdens, leading to low scores on experiential scales despite high objective risk.

Critical Challenges Identified:
  • Travel over 1 km to access water
  • Inconsistent primary water source
  • Need to supplement primary water source
  • High incidence of diarrheal illness

Recommendations

To better protect public health, assessment tools must be aligned with the physical and economic realities of communities like Dar es Salaam. Future applications of the HWISE scale should be modified or supplemented to capture:

  • Time and Distance: Explicitly accounting for the physical burden of travel.
  • Economic Burden: The financial impact of purchasing water.
  • Clinical Outcomes: Integrating the frequency of waterborne illnesses as a primary security indicator.
  • Long-term Resilience: Recognizing that communities with long histories of water challenges may under-report psychological distress despite environmental danger.
